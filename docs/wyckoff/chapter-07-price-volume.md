# Chapter 07

# Price and Volume

## Reading Market Evidence Instead of Market Signals

> *"Price tells us what happened.
>
> Volume tells us how much effort was required.
>
> Neither tells us what to do."*

---

# Chapter Overview

Nearly every technical analysis book

teaches investors

to interpret

price

and

volume.

Unfortunately,

most explanations

stop too early.

Readers memorize relationships such as

- Rising price with increasing volume is bullish.

- Falling price with increasing volume is bearish.

- Rising price with declining volume is weak.

These observations

are useful,

but incomplete.

Price

and

volume

are not investment signals.

They are evidence.

Evidence

must always

be interpreted

within context.

Throughout this chapter,

we will study

price and volume

not as indicators,

but as observable consequences

of institutional behaviour.

---

# Learning Objectives

After completing this chapter,

the reader should be able to

- Distinguish observations from conclusions.

- Explain why price alone is insufficient.

- Explain why volume alone is insufficient.

- Combine price and volume into evidence.

- Interpret market behaviour using institutional reasoning.

- Avoid common mistakes in signal-based trading.

---

# Historical Context

Richard D. Wyckoff

considered price

and volume

to be

the language

of the market.

He did not believe

that individual candles

predicted the future.

Instead,

he believed

that every movement

revealed information

about

the interaction

between supply

and demand.

The objective

was therefore

not prediction.

It was

interpretation.

This distinction

remains

one of the most misunderstood aspects

of Wyckoff's methodology.

# Section A

# Price and Volume Are Evidence

---

# 7.1 The Biggest Misunderstanding

Imagine observing

the following market.

```
Price

+4%
```

```
Volume

Very High
```

Many investors

immediately conclude

```
Bullish.
```

Professional investors

do not.

Instead,

they ask

```
Why

did price

rise?
```

The observation

contains

no explanation.

Price

records

the outcome.

It does not reveal

the cause.

---

## Observation Is Not Interpretation

This handbook

draws

a strict distinction

between

Observation

and

Interpretation.

For example,

```
Observation

Price

increased

4%.
```

This statement

is objective.

No opinion exists.

Now consider

```
Interpretation

Demand

became stronger.
```

This statement

contains

an inference.

It may be correct.

It may be wrong.

Evidence

always precedes

interpretation.

---

# 7.2 Price Is the Result

Price

is the final transaction.

Every completed trade

changes price.

Price therefore represents

the consequence

of thousands

of individual decisions.

Importantly,

price

does not reveal

who initiated

those decisions.

A five percent rally

could have resulted from

institutional buying,

retail enthusiasm,

short covering,

algorithmic execution,

or simply

temporary absence

of sellers.

The chart alone

cannot distinguish

between these possibilities.

Price

answers

"What happened?"

It does not answer

"Why?"

---

## Price Is Always Late

One of the most important principles

of this handbook

is the following.

Institutions

make decisions

before

price moves.

Investment Committee

↓

Capital Allocation

↓

Execution

↓

Transactions

↓

Price

Price

is therefore

the final visible consequence

of

earlier

institutional decisions.

This observation

explains

why price

should never be treated

as the beginning

of analysis.

---

# 7.3 Volume Is the Effort

If price

describes

the result,

volume

describes

the effort

required

to produce

that result.

Imagine pushing

a heavy truck.

Large effort.

Small movement.

Now imagine

pushing

an empty bicycle.

Small effort.

Large movement.

Markets

behave similarly.

Volume

represents

the effort.

Price movement

represents

the result.

Separately,

each provides

limited information.

Together,

they become meaningful.

---

## Volume Never Speaks Alone

High volume

does not automatically mean

institutions bought.

Nor does it prove

institutions sold.

High volume

simply indicates

that

many transactions occurred.

Interpretation

requires context.

Suppose

price rises

on exceptionally high volume.

Several explanations

remain possible.

- Institutions accumulated aggressively.

- Institutions distributed inventory.

- Retail investors entered simultaneously.

- Short sellers covered positions.

The volume itself

cannot distinguish

between these possibilities.

Reasoning

must continue.

---

# 7.4 Why Context Matters

Imagine hearing

someone shout

inside a building.

Without context,

the meaning

is impossible

to determine.

The person

may be celebrating.

They may be frightened.

They may simply be calling

to a friend.

Volume behaves

the same way.

Large volume

outside

Accumulation

means something different

from

large volume

inside

Distribution.

The observation

remains identical.

The context

changes completely.

---

## Evidence Requires Narrative

Professional investors

rarely evaluate

one candle.

Instead,

they examine

the story

developing

across many sessions.

Questions include

- Has volume increased

for several weeks?

- Is selling pressure weakening?

- Are pullbacks becoming smaller?

- Does demand continue expanding?

One observation

rarely changes

an investment thesis.

A sequence

of consistent observations

often does.

---

# 7.5 Evidence Before Conclusion

Suppose

the following event occurs.

```
Price

falls

8%.
```

```
Volume

Extremely Low.
```

Should the institution conclude

that

the market

has become bearish?

No.

A better question

might be

```
Why

did price

fall

despite

limited selling?
```

This question

opens

multiple hypotheses.

Perhaps

negative news

temporarily frightened

investors.

Perhaps

liquidity disappeared.

Perhaps

institutions

simply stopped buying

for one session.

Evidence

creates

questions.

Questions

create

hypotheses.

Hypotheses

guide

future observation.

Professional investing

therefore begins

not with answers,

but with better questions.

through

institutional reasoning.

meaningful investment decisions
