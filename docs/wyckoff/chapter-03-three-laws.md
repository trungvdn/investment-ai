# Overview

Every scientific discipline begins with a small number of fundamental principles.

Physics has Newton's Laws.

Thermodynamics has its own laws.

Economics has supply and demand.

Richard D. Wyckoff believed that financial markets could also be explained through a small number of universal principles.

Instead of hundreds of indicators,

Wyckoff proposed only three fundamental laws.

• Supply and Demand

• Cause and Effect

• Effort and Result

These three laws form the foundation of every market cycle.

Without understanding them,

Accumulation becomes pattern memorization.

Distribution becomes guesswork.

Spring becomes another chart pattern.

Understanding these three laws transforms technical analysis

into market reasoning.

## 3.1 Historical Context

Richard D. Wyckoff developed his methodology

during the early twentieth century.

The market looked very different.

There were

• no computers,

• no electronic trading,

• no ETFs,

• no algorithmic trading,

• no artificial intelligence.

Despite these differences,

human behavior remained unchanged.

Fear.

Greed.

Hope.

Panic.

Confidence.

These emotions continue to influence financial markets today.

Technology changes.

Human nature changes very little.

For this reason,

Wyckoff's three laws remain relevant

more than one hundred years later.


## 3.2 Why Only Three Laws?

One natural question immediately appears.

Why only three?

Why not ten?

Or twenty?

Wyckoff believed that

complex market behavior

could be explained

through a small number of universal relationships.

Every price movement

ultimately answers three questions.

Question One

Who is stronger?

Supply

or

Demand?

Question Two

Is today's movement

consistent

with today's effort?

Question Three

Has enough preparation occurred

to justify

the next large movement?

These questions become

the Three Laws.


## 3.3 The Relationship Between The Three Laws

One common misunderstanding

is believing that

the three laws

operate independently.

They do not.

Instead,

they describe

the same market

from different perspectives.

Imagine observing

a construction site.

Supply vs Demand

answers

Who is currently moving materials?

Effort vs Result

answers

Are workers producing expected progress?

Cause vs Effect

answers

Has enough foundation been built

to support

the next stage?

All three observations

describe

the same project.

The market works exactly the same way.
